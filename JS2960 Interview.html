2. How do you understand the concept of "digital humanities"?

Digital humanities represents the convergence of traditional humanities disciplines with digital tools and methodologies. It's not simply about using technology to study humanities topics, but rather creating new ways of understanding human culture, history, and society through computational methods.

I see digital humanities as having three key dimensions: first, using digital tools to analyze cultural artifacts and texts in ways previously impossible; second, creating digital platforms and resources that make humanities knowledge more accessible; and third, critically examining how technology itself shapes human experience and society.

What makes this field exciting is its potential to democratize knowledge, reveal hidden patterns in cultural data, and bridge the gap between academic research and public engagement. It's about asking age-old human questions with new technological capabilities.

The courses in this concentration, such as "AI and Good Life: Global Perspectives" and "Public Policy and Governance," align perfectly with these interests.

4. Can you describe a time when you've combined technology with creative or analytical thinking?

During my high school history project on local heritage, I created a digital storytelling platform that combined historical research with interactive technology. Instead of writing a traditional research paper, I developed a multimedia presentation using web technologies to tell the story of my community's development over the past century.

I collected oral histories from elderly residents, digitized old photographs and documents, and used mapping software to visualize how the neighborhood changed over time. The most challenging part was finding a way to present complex historical relationships in an accessible format, which led me to create an interactive timeline where users could explore different themes – social, economic, and cultural changes.

This project taught me that technology isn't just a tool for efficiency; it's a medium for creative expression and critical analysis. It also showed me how digital methods can make humanities research more engaging and accessible to broader audiences, which is exactly what I hope to explore further in this program.

5. What do you think are the main challenges facing society in our digital age?

I believe we face several interconnected challenges in our digital age. First, there's the growing digital divide – not just in terms of access to technology, but in digital literacy and the ability to critically evaluate online information. This creates inequalities in education, employment opportunities, and civic participation.

Second, we're grappling with questions of privacy, surveillance, and data ownership. As our lives become increasingly digitized, we need to balance the benefits of personalized services and data-driven innovations with fundamental rights to privacy and autonomy.

Third, there's the challenge of maintaining human connection and empathy in an increasingly digital world. Social media and digital communication offer wonderful opportunities for global connection, but they can also contribute to polarization, misinformation, and social isolation.

Finally, we face the challenge of ensuring that artificial intelligence and automation serve human flourishing rather than replacing human judgment and creativity. This requires thoughtful integration of technology with humanistic values and ethical considerations.

6. How do you see AI impacting the humanities field?

AI is transforming humanities in fascinating ways. It's enabling us to analyze vast corpora of texts, identify patterns across centuries of literature, and uncover connections that would be impossible to find through traditional close reading alone. For example, AI can help track the evolution of concepts across different cultures and time periods.

However, I believe AI's most important contribution to humanities isn't just analytical – it's raising fundamental questions about what makes us human. As AI systems become more sophisticated in language generation and creative tasks, we're forced to reconsider concepts like authorship, creativity, and meaning-making.

There's also tremendous potential for AI to democratize humanities education by providing personalized learning experiences and making rare manuscripts and artifacts digitally accessible to scholars worldwide.

At the same time, we need humanistic perspectives to guide AI development ethically. The humanities' tradition of critical thinking and ethical reflection is essential for ensuring that AI serves human values and doesn't perpetuate biases or replace human judgment where it's needed most.

7. Tell us about a time when you worked on a collaborative project. What was your role and what did you learn?

Last year, I participated in a school-wide sustainability initiative where our team of six students from different disciplines was tasked with developing a proposal to reduce campus waste. My role was research coordinator and communications liaison.

As research coordinator, I organized our fact-finding efforts, ensuring we gathered data on current waste patterns, researched best practices from other institutions, and analyzed the cost-benefit of different solutions. As communications liaison, I helped facilitate discussions between team members with very different backgrounds – science students, art students, and business students – ensuring everyone's perspectives were heard and integrated.

What I learned was the incredible value of diverse perspectives. The science students provided technical expertise, the art students contributed creative visualization and communication strategies, and the business students ensured our proposals were economically viable. My role was often to translate between these different "languages" and help the team synthesize ideas.

This experience taught me that the most innovative solutions emerge when people with different expertise collaborate effectively. It also showed me the importance of having someone who can bridge different disciplinary perspectives – which is exactly what the Digital Futures and Humanities program prepares students to do.

11. Describe a situation where you had to adapt to a significant change or challenge.

The most significant adaptation I've had to make was during the COVID-19 pandemic when our entire educational experience shifted online overnight. Initially, I struggled with the loss of face-to-face interaction and the different dynamics of online learning.

Rather than just accepting the situation, I decided to actively explore how to make online learning more effective. I experimented with different digital tools for note-taking and collaboration, organized virtual study groups with classmates, and even started tutoring younger students online to help them adapt as well.

What surprised me was discovering that digital tools could sometimes enhance learning – for instance, being able to record discussions for later review, or using collaborative documents for real-time group work. I also learned about digital equity issues when some classmates struggled with internet access or appropriate devices.

This experience taught me that technological disruption often reveals both new possibilities and new inequalities. It reinforced my interest in understanding how digital technologies can be implemented thoughtfully to maximize benefits while addressing potential harms – exactly the kind of thinking this program promotes.

12. What role do you think culture plays in shaping technology adoption?

Culture profoundly shapes how technologies are adopted, adapted, and integrated into daily life. Different societies have varying attitudes toward privacy, community, authority, and innovation that influence which technologies succeed and how they're used.

For example, the success of mobile payment systems like Alipay and WeChat Pay in China versus the slower adoption in other countries reflects different cultural attitudes toward financial institutions, digital security, and convenience. Similarly, social media platforms develop differently across cultures based on communication norms and relationship structures.

Culture also influences what problems people want technology to solve. In some societies, there's strong emphasis on individual efficiency and productivity, while others prioritize community connection and collective benefit. These values shape which innovations gain traction and how they evolve.

Understanding these cultural dimensions is crucial for anyone working in technology policy or innovation management. We can't assume that technologies will have the same impact or acceptance across different cultural contexts. This is why the program's emphasis on transcultural competence is so valuable.

13. How would you approach a project that requires both technical skills and creative problem-solving?

I would begin by clearly understanding the problem from multiple perspectives – not just the technical requirements, but the human needs, cultural context, and broader implications. This involves stakeholder interviews, user research, and understanding the ecosystem in which the solution will operate.

Next, I'd use creative techniques like mind mapping and scenario planning to explore different possible approaches before committing to technical solutions. I find it helpful to separate the ideation phase from the feasibility assessment – first generating many possibilities, then evaluating them against technical, financial, and social constraints.

Throughout the development process, I would maintain close contact with end users, using iterative design principles to test and refine solutions. I believe the best technical solutions emerge from ongoing dialogue between technical possibilities and human needs.

Finally, I'd ensure that the evaluation criteria include not just technical performance, but also user experience, cultural appropriateness, and broader social impact. The most elegant technical solution isn't necessarily the best solution if it doesn't serve real human needs effectively.

14. What ethical considerations do you think are most important when working with digital technologies?

I believe the most fundamental ethical consideration is ensuring that digital technologies enhance rather than diminish human agency and dignity. This means designing systems that empower people to make informed decisions rather than manipulating them through dark patterns or algorithmic persuasion.

Privacy and data protection are crucial, but I think we need to go beyond just consent mechanisms to consider how data collection affects power relationships and social structures. We should ask: does this technology increase or decrease inequality? Does it strengthen or weaken democratic participation?

Algorithmic fairness is another key area – ensuring that AI systems don't perpetuate or amplify existing biases. This requires diverse teams, inclusive design processes, and ongoing monitoring of real-world outcomes, not just technical performance metrics.

Finally, I think we need to consider long-term and systemic effects, not just immediate individual impacts. How do digital technologies change social norms, economic structures, and political processes? These broader considerations are where humanities perspectives become essential to technological development.

15. How do you handle situations where you need to learn something completely new?

When facing completely new material, I start by building a conceptual framework – understanding the basic vocabulary, key principles, and how this new area relates to things I already know. I find that creating visual concept maps helps me organize new information and identify connections.

I also believe in learning by doing whenever possible. Rather than just reading about new concepts, I try to find hands-on projects or real-world applications where I can test my understanding. This approach helps me identify gaps in my knowledge early and makes the learning more engaging.

Seeking out diverse perspectives is crucial – I'll read academic sources, watch video tutorials, join online communities, and if possible, talk to practitioners who work in the field. Different explanations often illuminate different aspects of complex topics.

Most importantly, I've learned to be comfortable with initial confusion and uncertainty. Complex fields can't be mastered immediately, so I focus on making steady progress rather than expecting immediate expertise. I also try to teach or explain concepts to others, which often reveals areas where my understanding needs strengthening.

17. Can you give an example of how you've used technology to solve a problem creatively?

During the pandemic, I noticed that many elderly people in my community were becoming increasingly isolated, especially those who weren't comfortable with video calling technology. Traditional solutions like teaching them to use Zoom weren't working because the interfaces were too complex and intimidating.

I created a simplified communication system using basic smartphones and pre-programmed contacts. Instead of requiring them to navigate complex menus, I set up one-touch calling and messaging systems with large, clearly labeled buttons. I also created simple instruction cards with visual guides.

The key insight was recognizing that the problem wasn't just technical – it was about confidence and fear of making mistakes. So I also established a peer support network where early adopters could help others, and created a simple troubleshooting system with local volunteers.

This project taught me that effective technological solutions require understanding not just the technical requirements, but also the social and psychological barriers to adoption. The best solutions often involve simplifying rather than adding features.

19. How do you balance different perspectives when they conflict?

When facing conflicting perspectives, I start by trying to understand the underlying values and assumptions behind each viewpoint. Often, disagreements stem from different priorities rather than factual disputes – for example, valuing innovation versus stability, or individual privacy versus collective benefit.

I find it helpful to look for common ground and shared goals, even when the approaches differ. For instance, in debates about AI regulation, most parties want beneficial outcomes – they just disagree on the best methods to achieve them.

I also try to consider whose voices might be missing from the discussion. Sometimes conflicts arise because certain stakeholder perspectives aren't being adequately represented. Bringing in those missing viewpoints can often reveal new solutions.

When genuine conflicts remain, I believe in transparent decision-making processes that acknowledge trade-offs rather than pretending they don't exist. The goal isn't always to eliminate conflict, but to ensure that decisions are made thoughtfully with full consideration of different impacts and perspectives.
